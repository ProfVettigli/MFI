The Mystery of Nicolas Bourbaki

Discover the story of the greatest mathematician of the 20th century... who never actually existed. Uncover the secret society that rewrote modern mathematics.

1. The Man Who Never Was

In the mid-1930s, a brilliant new mathematician named Nicolas Bourbaki began publishing highly advanced mathematical papers. His work was characterized by absolute, uncompromising rigor and a deeply abstract axiomatic approach. He supposedly resided in "Nancago" (a fictional blend of Nancy and Chicago) and even applied for membership in the American Mathematical Society.

There was only one problem: Nicolas Bourbaki was completely fake.

The Secret Society

Bourbaki was actually the collective pseudonym for a secret society of young, rebellious French mathematicians. Formed in 1934 in a café in Paris (\(Café \ A. \ Capoulade\)), the founding members included titans of modern math like André Weil, Henri Cartan, Claude Chevalley, and Jean Dieudonné. They were frustrated by the outdated, disorganized mathematical textbooks used in universities at the time, and decided to take matters into their own hands.

2. The Prank That Started It All

But why the strange name "Bourbaki"? The answer lies in an incredibly funny anecdote from the École Normale Supérieure in Paris.

A few years earlier, in 1923, an older student named Raoul Husson decided to play a practical joke on the freshers. He disguised himself as a visiting foreign professor, gathered the first-year math students, and passionately presented an entirely fake, nonsensical proof filled with absurd equations. He proudly concluded the lecture by attributing this fake theorem to a fictional Russian general named "Bourbaki" (inspired by a real 19th-century French general, Charles Denis Bourbaki, who suffered a tragic defeat).

When the secret society of mathematicians was formed years later, they remembered this legendary prank and decided to adopt "Nicolas Bourbaki" as their collective alter-ego as a tribute.

3. The Goal: Rewriting Mathematics

Bourbaki's mission was titanic: to rewrite all of mathematics from scratch, basing it entirely on strict Set Theory (Teoria degli Insiemi) and avoiding any reliance on visual, geometric intuition which they considered "unreliable".

They began publishing a massive series of books called "Éléments de mathématique". Their rules for collaboration were fierce: every single word had to be unanimously agreed upon by all members. Their meetings often devolved into members screaming theories at each other across the room for hours. To ensure the group remained innovative, a strict rule forced any member to retire at age 50.

What We Owe To Bourbaki

Although their extremely dry and rigorous approach is sometimes criticized today for being too abstract for beginners, Bourbaki successfully standardized the international language of modern mathematics. You are probably using their inventions without knowing it!

  • The Symbol \(\emptyset\): They invented the slashed zero symbol to represent the Empty Set.
  • Arrows in Functions (\(\rightarrow\)): The ubiquitous notation \(f: A \rightarrow B\) was popularized by them.
  • Terminology: They coined the fundamental terms Injective, Surjective, and Bijective for classifying functions.
  • The "Dangerous Bend" Symbol (☡): Used in the margins of their books to warn readers of tricky concepts or common mistakes!

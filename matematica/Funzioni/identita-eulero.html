👤 Who Was Leonhard Euler?

Leonhard Euler (1707–1783) was a Swiss mathematician and physicist, widely regarded as the most prolific mathematician in history. He worked in virtually every branch of mathematics — number theory, graph theory, mechanics, optics, and astronomy — and his collected works fill over 80 volumes.

Euler introduced most of the mathematical notation we still use today: the symbol \(e\) for the base of the natural logarithm, \(i\) for the imaginary unit, \(\pi\) for the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, \(f(x)\) for a function, and the capital sigma \(\Sigma\) for summation.

💡

Remarkable fact: Euler lost the sight of his right eye in 1738 and became completely blind in 1771 — yet he continued to produce groundbreaking mathematics at an even faster rate than before, dictating his work to assistants. He once said: "Now I will have fewer distractions."

📚

Scale of his output: It has been estimated that Euler wrote, on average, 800 pages of mathematics per year over his entire career. The Saint Petersburg Academy of Sciences was still publishing his previously unprinted manuscripts 47 years after his death.

Section 2

📜 The History of the Formula

Euler's Identity is a special case of a much more general result: Euler's Formula. Understanding how this formula was discovered requires following the trail of some of the most daring ideas in the history of mathematics.

~1545 — Imaginary Numbers Are Born
Gerolamo Cardano encountered square roots of negative numbers while solving cubic equations. Mathematicians of his era found these "impossible" numbers deeply unsettling, yet couldn't deny their usefulness. René Descartes later sneered at them, calling them "imaginary" — a sarcastic label that stuck.
1614–1659 — Logarithms & the Number e
John Napier invented logarithms in 1614, revolutionising astronomy and navigation. The number \(e \approx 2.71828\ldots\) emerged naturally from the study of compound interest and logarithms, first appearing in letters by Jacob Bernoulli in 1683.
1690s — Complex Numbers Are Accepted
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz and Johan Bernoulli debated the logarithms of negative and complex numbers, noticing strange connections between trigonometry and exponentials — but couldn't fully explain them.
1714 — Roger Cotes' Glimpse
English mathematician Roger Cotes wrote, perhaps for the first time, something close to Euler's formula in a different notation: \( \ln(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = i\theta \). He died at 33 before fully developing the idea.
1748 — Euler Publishes Introductio in analysin infinitorum
Leonhard Euler derives the complete formula \( e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x \) in his masterwork. Setting \( x = \pi \) immediately yields the identity \( e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 \). Euler grasped the formula's full significance and placed it at the centre of complex analysis.
19th–20th Century — The Formula's Fame Grows
The identity gained legendary status. In 1988, the mathematical journal Mathematical Intelligencer ran a poll asking readers to vote for the most beautiful theorem in mathematics. Euler's Identity came first. A 2004 poll in Physics World asked physicists: same result.
Section 3

🔢 Meet the Five Constants

What makes Euler's Identity so astonishing is that it unites five of the most fundamental constants in mathematics, each arising from a completely different area — and yet they fit together perfectly.

e

Euler's Number

\(e \approx 2.71828\ldots\)
The base of natural logarithms. Governs exponential growth and decay, compound interest, probability.

i

Imaginary Unit

\(i = \sqrt{-1}\)
The foundation of complex numbers. "Impossible" yet essential in engineering, physics, and signal processing.

π

Pi

\(\pi \approx 3.14159\ldots\)
The ratio of circumference to diameter of any circle. Appears everywhere in geometry, waves, and statistics.

1

One

The multiplicative identity. The building block of all counting numbers and arithmetic.

0

Zero

The additive identity. One of humanity's greatest mathematical inventions — nothing made into something.

Euler's Identity: \( e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 \)

This single equation links two fundamental operations (addition and exponentiation), two identities (the additive identity 0 and the multiplicative identity 1), and three transcendental/imaginary constants (\(e\), \(i\), \(\pi\)) — each discovered centuries apart, in completely different contexts.
Section 4

🌍 The G5 Summit of Mathematics

You have probably heard of the G7 — the annual summit where the leaders of the world's seven major economies gather at the same table to discuss global affairs. It is considered extraordinary precisely because it is so rare to have all these powerful figures in the same room at the same time.

🏛️

Think of Euler's Identity as the G5 summit of mathematics: the five most important numbers in all of mathematics — each a superpower in its own domain — sitting together at the same table, united by a single, impossibly elegant equation.

\(e\) represents analysis and growth, \(i\) represents the complex plane, \(\pi\) represents geometry and cycles, \(1\) represents arithmetic, and \(0\) represents the concept of nothingness. The fact that they all appear in one formula, with no extra coefficients, no clutter, no approximations — just pure equality — is what makes mathematicians go silent in awe.

The physicist Richard Feynman — one of the greatest scientific minds of the 20th century and no stranger to beautiful ideas — called it "the most remarkable formula in mathematics." He first encountered it as a teenager and reportedly stared at it for hours.

🎓

Stanford mathematician Keith Devlin put it this way: "Like a Shakespearean sonnet that captures the very essence of love, or a painting that brings out the beauty of the human form that is far more than just skin deep, Euler's equation reaches down into the very depths of existence."

Section 5

⚙️ Euler's Formula — The Building Block

Euler's Identity is actually a special case of the more general Euler's Formula, which connects the complex exponential to trigonometry:

\[ e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x \quad \text{for any real number } x \]

This formula tells us that raising \(e\) to an imaginary power produces a rotation in the complex plane. When \(x\) increases from \(0\) to \(2\pi\), the point \(e^{ix}\) traces out a complete circle of radius 1 — the unit circle.

Deriving the Identity

Simply substitute \(x = \pi\) into Euler's Formula:

\[ e^{i\pi} = \cos\pi + i\sin\pi = -1 + i \cdot 0 = -1 \]

Therefore:

\[ e^{i\pi} = -1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 \]
🔄

Key insight: \(\cos\pi = -1\) because \(\pi\) radians is a half-turn — exactly halfway around the unit circle — which lands you at the point \((-1, 0)\). And \(\sin\pi = 0\) because that point lies on the real axis. The imaginary part vanishes, leaving just \(-1\).

Where Does Euler's Formula Come From?

The deepest proof uses Taylor series — infinite polynomial expansions. We expand \(e^{ix}\), \(\cos x\), and \(\sin x\):

\[ e^{ix} = 1 + ix + \frac{(ix)^2}{2!} + \frac{(ix)^3}{3!} + \frac{(ix)^4}{4!} + \cdots \]

Using \(i^2 = -1\), \(i^3 = -i\), \(i^4 = 1\) and separating real from imaginary terms gives exactly \(\cos x + i\sin x\). The two seemingly unrelated worlds — exponentials and trigonometry — turn out to be the same function, seen from different angles.

📝

Note for students: Taylor series and complex numbers are topics you will study in depth in future years. For now, what matters is the result: a complex exponential is a rotation, and \(x = \pi\) rotates exactly halfway around the circle, landing on −1.

✨ Why Is It So Beautiful?

Mathematical beauty is akin to aesthetic beauty: partly subjective, partly universal. When mathematicians say Euler's Identity is beautiful, they are pointing to several distinct qualities.

The Criteria of Mathematical Beauty

1. Economy — It says everything using nothing

The equation \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\) contains only seven symbols (five constants, one operation, one relation) and zero superfluous elements. No coefficients. No approximation sign. No fine print. This is the mathematical equivalent of a haiku — the maximum of meaning in the minimum of form.

2. Unexpectedness — Nobody expected this

\(e\) comes from calculus and continuous growth. \(\pi\) comes from geometry and circles. \(i\) comes from algebra and the desire to solve \(x^2 = -1\). These areas have nothing obvious in common. And yet they are secretly the same thing, connected through the complex exponential. The surprise is key to beauty — the moment of "How can this be true?!"

3. Depth — It reveals hidden structure

The identity is not a coincidence or a trick — it reflects a deep structural truth about mathematics: rotation and exponential growth are the same operation, just applied in different directions. Real exponents produce growth; imaginary exponents produce rotation. The formula shows that geometry and analysis are two faces of the same coin.

4. Inevitability — Once you see it, it couldn't be otherwise

After Euler's Formula is established, the identity feels inevitable: of course \(e^{i\pi} = -1\). Of course it was always true. This sense of "it couldn't be any other way" is what the philosopher Bertrand Russell described as the cold beauty of mathematics — logic so tight that there is no room for it to be wrong.

🎵

An analogy: the identity is to mathematics what Beethoven's Fifth Symphony is to music. Not because it is complex, but because it achieves perfection through simplicity — a brief, inevitable statement that contains infinite depth.

Section 8

💬 Famous Reactions & Anecdotes

Richard Feynman, age 14

Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman first encountered Euler's Formula from a library book as a teenager. He later wrote in his notebooks: "The most remarkable formula in mathematics" — a phrase he never retracted. When asked to explain it, he would often pause, smile, and say: "If you're not already amazed, you haven't understood it yet."

The Tattoo Test

A famous anecdote in mathematics departments is the "tattoo test": it is said that \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\) is the most common tattoo among mathematicians and physicists — ahead of any other equation, including \(E = mc^2\). Whether or not this is statistically true, the story captures something real: the formula inspires a kind of personal, almost spiritual devotion.

Benjamin Peirce's Reaction (1850s)

American mathematician Benjamin Peirce — one of the leading figures of 19th-century American mathematics — reportedly wrote the formula on a blackboard in front of his Harvard students, then turned around and said:

🖊️

"Gentlemen, that is surely true, it is absolutely paradoxical; we cannot understand it, and we don't know what it means. But we have proved it, and therefore we know it must be the truth."

Mathematics in Pop Culture

Euler's Identity has appeared in countless novels, films, and TV shows as a shorthand for genius or mathematical beauty. In the TV series Futurama, writers (many of whom hold advanced mathematics degrees) embedded it in an episode as a visual joke. In the film The Avengers, a character's chalkboard famously features it. It has even been referenced in songs and architecture. A formula from 1748 — still culturally relevant today.

Why Euler Got the Credit (and Not Cotes)

Roger Cotes came closest before Euler, but his version was obscured in notation — he didn't fully realise the geometric and algebraic significance of what he had found. Euler, by contrast, recognised the result, understood it deeply, and placed it in the context of a general theory. In mathematics, credit goes not just to whoever writes something first, but to whoever understands it fully and communicates it clearly.

🔭

Applications today: Euler's Formula (the general form) is everywhere in modern science and technology — it underpins signal processing (the Fourier Transform), quantum mechanics (the wave function), electrical engineering (AC circuit analysis), and computer graphics (3D rotations). The "beautiful but useless" equation turns out to be one of the most useful equations ever written.

Évariste Galois: The Mathematical Rebel

Discover the life of the young genius who changed algebra forever before dying at just 20 years old.

1. A Life of Fire and Logic

Évariste Galois (1811–1832) was a French mathematician and political activist. Unlike many other famous scientists, Galois was a rebel. He was twice rejected by the École Polytechnique, arrested for his political beliefs, and lived a life marked by frustration and brilliance.

Even though he died at the age of 20, his work laid the foundation for Modern Algebra.

2. Mathematical Challenges: The Battle of Wits

For centuries, mathematics was a competitive arena. Mathematicians did not just publish papers; they challenged each other to public duels. They would exchange a list of complex problems, and the winner was the one who solved the highest number of their opponent's riddles.

These battles were about money, jobs, and above all, honor. Possessing a secret formula—a method that no one else knew—was like having a secret weapon. It provided a massive strategic advantage, much like how the development of the atomic bomb gave the United States a decisive edge in global conflict.

The Cubic War: Cardano vs. Tartaglia

Solving equations was the most common type of challenge. The most famous "war" was over the Cubic Equation (\(x^3 + px = q\)):

  • 📅 1530-1535: Niccolò Tartaglia, a self-taught genius, discovers a method to solve certain cubic equations. In a public duel in 1535, he solves 30 problems in a row, humiliating his rival!
  • 📅 1539: Gerolamo Cardano, a polymath, begs Tartaglia for his secret. Tartaglia eventually shares it as a poem, but only after making Cardano swear an oath of absolute secrecy.
  • 📅 1545: Breaking his vow, Cardano publishes the formula in his masterpiece Ars Magna. He justifies this because he found that Scipione del Ferro had discovered it even earlier.
  • 📅 1548: A final, legendary duel takes place between Tartaglia and Luigi Ferrari (Cardano's student). The result was a disaster for Tartaglia, cementing Cardano's legacy forever.

3. What did he discover?

∄

The Insolvability Proof

While everyone was searching for a formula for 5th-degree equations, Galois did the unthinkable. He used Group Theory to prove that a general formula using only radicals (square roots, etc.) cannot exist for equations of degree 5 or higher. He didn't just fail to find it—he proved it was impossible.

Note: The "Prince" Gauss's perspective.
While Galois showed we can't always find a simple formula, the great Carl Friedrich Gauss proved in his Fundamental Theorem of Algebra that solutions always exist! He demonstrated that an equation of degree \(n\) always has exactly \(n\) solutions, though some might be complex numbers (not real).

🌀

Symmetry and Groups

By studying how the roots of an equation can be swapped (permuted), he invented Group Theory. This is now used in everything from modern physics to the encryption that secures your smartphone!

4. The Final Duel

In May 1832, Galois was challenged to a real duel with pistols. The reason is still debated: a woman, political enemies, or a hopeless "suicide by honor"?

"I have not time! I have not time!"

The night before the duel, sensing his death, he stayed up until dawn writing down all his mathematical theories. In the margins, he feverishly wrote "Je n'ai pas le temps".

The next morning, he was shot in the stomach and died the following day. He was only 20.

Vectors in Video Games

Discover how the Cartesian Plane, coordinate systems, and vector operations make it possible for your favourite video game characters to move, jump, and live on screen.

1. The Screen is a Cartesian Plane

Every video game you play — from Super Mario Bros. to Fortnite — runs on the same fundamental idea: the screen is a Coordinate System. Each pixel on the screen has a unique address defined by two numbers: an X coordinate (horizontal position) and a Y coordinate (vertical position).

When a game developer writes the code for a character like Mario, they don't tell him "go right". Instead, they say something like: "increase your X position by 5 pixels every frame". The entire world of the game exists as numbers inside a coordinate plane.

⚠️ Screen Coordinates vs. Math Coordinates

In mathematics, the Y-axis points upward. But in computer graphics, the origin \((0, 0)\) is typically at the top-left corner of the screen, and the Y-axis points downward. This means that increasing Y moves objects down, not up! Game developers must always keep this difference in mind.

3. Velocity is a Vector

In physics and in video games, velocity is not just a number — it is a vector. It has both a magnitude (how fast?) and a direction (where?). A game character stores its velocity as a pair of components:

Velocity Vector \(\vec{v} = (v_x, \, v_y)\) \(v_x\) = horizontal speed, \(v_y\) = vertical speed

Every single frame (typically 60 times per second!), the game engine updates the character's position using the simplest yet most powerful equation in game development:

The Game Loop Update Rule \(\begin{cases} x_{\text{new}} = x_{\text{old}} + v_x \cdot \Delta t \\ y_{\text{new}} = y_{\text{old}} + v_y \cdot \Delta t \end{cases}\) where \(\Delta t\) is the time elapsed since the last frame

5. Vector Addition: Gravity and Jumps

What happens when a character jumps? The game sets an initial upward velocity \(\vec{v}_{\text{jump}} = (0, -v_0)\) (negative because up means smaller Y in screen coordinates). But at every frame, gravity adds a small downward vector to the velocity:

Gravity Applied Every Frame \(\vec{v}_{\text{new}} = \vec{v}_{\text{old}} + \vec{g} \cdot \Delta t\) where \(\vec{g} = (0, +g)\) points downward on screen

This simple vector addition is what creates the beautiful parabolic arc of a jump. The horizontal component stays constant (no air friction), while the vertical component starts negative (going up), gradually reaches zero (peak of the jump), and then becomes positive (falling back down). It's the same projectile motion you study in physics!

🎮 Fun Fact

In the original Super Mario Bros. (1985), gravity is set to approximately 75 pixels/s². But when Mario is falling, Nintendo secretly increases gravity to make the game feel more responsive. This "cheat" makes the controls feel incredibly satisfying — and it's all just tweaking a vector!
